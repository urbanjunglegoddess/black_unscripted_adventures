The Weight We Carry That Was Never Ours

Community Contributor

Sharing stories from the heart of the Afro-Diaspora — unfiltered, unscripted, and unapologetically real.

Black Unscripted Adventures

There's a kind of tired that sleep doesn't fix. You know the one — where you wake up already carrying something heavy, and you can't quite name it, but it has been there your whole life. It lives in the way you tense up in certain rooms, the way you over-explain yourself before anyone has questioned you, the way you apologize for taking up space.

Generational trauma is real. And the first step toward releasing it is recognizing that not all of what we feel originated with us. Some of it was handed down — through silence, through survival, through the stories our grandparents couldn't tell out loud.

Naming What Was Never Named

  • Healing starts with language. When we name the pain — not to perform it, but to understand it — we begin to separate ourselves from it. You are not the wound. You are the one who can look at it.

Identifying What Isn't Yours

  • Ask yourself: where did this belief come from? Was it taught? Was it absorbed from watching someone you loved shrink themselves? The goal isn't blame — it's clarity.
  • Some fears protected our ancestors. They don't need to protect us in the same way anymore. We can honor where they came from while choosing not to carry them forward.

The Practice of Putting It Down

  • This isn't a one-time event. It's a practice — like stretching a muscle that has been clenched too long. Some days you'll pick the weight back up without realizing it. That's human. The practice is noticing, and choosing again.

Why Community Matters in Healing

  • We were not meant to heal alone. Our culture has always known this — the village, the circle, the congregation. When one person speaks their truth and another recognizes it, something shifts. You are not alone in this. That's why this space exists.
  • Black Unscripted Adventures is built on the belief that our stories, shared honestly, become medicine. Not just for the person sharing — but for everyone who reads and finally exhales.
